The first real thing that I had to do using the arduino kit was create some basic circuits. We had already learned about the parts of a circuit as well as Ohm's law in class, so this was an exercise in putting what I learned into practice.

Babyâ€™s first circuit

The first cricut I had to make was a pretty simple one. It has one button that must be pressed to turn the LED on. I gathered all my materials out of the bags to get started.

Then I followed the video instructions to make the circuit. Starting by using the red wire for the 5v and the black wire for the ground. From there I placed the button and the LED making sure the button was the correct way. I then connected the button to the 5V power with a resistor to prevent the LED from receiving too much voltage. From there, I used more wires to complete the circuit. I made sure to connect the anode side of the LED to the incoming power, and the cathode side to the ground wire.

Then I plugged it in. And it worked! First circuit was a success.

I can see here from the drawing in the book how exactly the circuit works. The power comes from the 5V wire, then runs through a resistor which is connected to a button that does not complete the circuit until pushed down on. It then flows to an LED light, then to the ground where the full circuit is completed.

Series circuit

The second circuit I needed to make was a series circuit. As shown in this model here, it is similar to the first one but has two buttons instead. The buttons are in a row meaning both buttons need to be pushed down in order for the LED to light up.

I slightly change the first circuit by adding another button, then using wires to complete the circuit. Making sure to connect the cathode side of the LED to the ground and so on.

Once again it worked exactly as intended by only lighting up when both buttons were pressed. If only one button was pressed the circuit would not be complete and would not light up the LED. But when both were pressed it lit up!

Parallel circuit

The last circuit I had to make was the parallel circuit. Like the series circuit, it also has two buttons, but you don't have to push both buttons to get the LED to light up. As shown in the diagram here, the circuit will be complete if at least one button is pressed.

I first connected the wires to make sure the first button created a loop. Then I connected the second button and made sure that also created a full look for the electricity to flow through.

Circuit complete! Pressing either button down, or both buttons at the same time causes the LED to light up exactly as it should.

Conclusion
